We thank the referee for their helpful comments. We respond to their suggestions below and provide some clarifying remarks. Edits to the manuscript are highlighted with red text.

- First, we would like to emphasize that the mock catalogs in our study all contain 400 events, not 10^6 -- 10^6 is the number of detectable sources that we find from a fixed population and use to compute the expected number of detections from other populations during inference, i.e., the term N_exp(R) in the population likelihood (see Sections IIA and IIB). We have added a sentence just before Section IVA that hopefully makes this clear.

- The referee has made excellent suggestions for follow-up work and we have already started exploring exactly those suggestions. In particular, we are applying the PixelPop model to populations in which correlations exist in three or four dimensions, investigating the impact of assuming only lower-dimensional correlations and the resulting biased inference. In particular, we are targeting correlations between primary mass, mass ratio, redshift, and effective spin, just as the referee suggests. We prefer leaving this to a followup paper as this submission to PRD is primarily focused on describing the method / model and applying it to a first test case -- which already has resulted in a long and technical paper which we do not want to complicate further!

- A discussion on applying PixelPop to higher dimensions is already found in the Conclusions. We are also investigating improvements in computational efficiency via sparse matrix representations and reducing our model to the intrinsic autoregressive model in which kappa=1, and added a sentence mentioning this to the Conclusions.

- The prior on the CAR model parameters does not have a strong influence. In particular, kappa strongly favors one (also why we are considering the intrinsic autoregressive model instead). In this limit, the mean rate parameter mu also does not influence the likelihood -- see Eq. (14) -- in which case the posterior for it does reduce to the prior. On the other hand, the variance parameter, sigma, is well constrained away from the prior. We have added text to point this out in Section IIIB.

- The posterior in Fig. 9 is fully consistent with the true value, but the main reason the MAP value is offset is the influence of the prior -- one can see with the dashed curve that this prior heavily disfavors large values of the correlation and broadening coefficients. We mention this in the last paragraph of Section IIIC. We have added an additional description of this effect at the end if Section IVB.

- We have also already prepared a data release for the updated version of the papers, which can be found out https://zenodo.org/doi/10.5281/zenodo.13176115. We have added a reference to this in the Introductions and Conclusions.
