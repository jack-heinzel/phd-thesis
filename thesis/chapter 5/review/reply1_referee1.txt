We thank the Referee for their helpful comments and for taking the time to review our manuscript. We respond to their concerns below. We also attach this reply as a .txt file. Edits to the manuscript are highlighted with red text in the PDF.

> "My main concern is with the conclusion that the non-parametric models recover the true distribution, whereas the parametric models do not. Part of this argument relies on examining the posterior distribution of Xeff. The authors focus on the positive region (paragraph beginning on line 385), where it is indeed the case that the parametric reconstruction fails to capture the true behavior. On the negative side of the distribution, Fig. 4 shows that the parametric model reproduces the true distribution almost perfectly, whereas Fig. 5 indicates that the non-parametric model performs noticeably worse."

The Referee raises a reasonable concern: it is indeed true that in some regions of parameter space (e.g., the negative side of the Xeff distribution), the Peak+Tukey parametric model recovers the truth better than PixelPop. 

However, it's important to note that the comparison is being made in an unrealistic scenario in which the parametric model can be chosen to match the known form of the population synthesis simulations. In this idealized case, since such a parametric model must match the truth in data-rich regions, it automatically has to perform well in regions with few/no detections. But this does not imply that the information about the region with low rate is mostly coming from that same region. 

More importantly, in all realistic cases, we would not be able to set up a model that can perfectly match the truth for some value of its parameters, and in these cases, biases could arise anywhere. For example, analyzing our population-synthesis informed dataset with the default LVK parametric model for Xeff, i.e., a Gaussian (see Abac, A. G., et al. "GWTC-4.0: Population properties of merging compact binaries." arXiv:2508.18083), we would see severe model misspecification (see the purple line in the attached Fig. 1), even in the data-informed regions. 

This is one of the reasons we consider approaches like PixelPop advantageous relative to parametric models, which usually offer less flexibility and may be prone to misspecification. Moreover, our parametric model only matches the marginal effective-spin distribution, not the correlations with other source properties; and non-trivial high-dimensional correlations are hard to implement in parametric models.

We have included in lines 483-491 some language to acknowledge that the parametric model recovers some features of the true population better than PixelPop (e.g., the asymmetry of the Xeff distribution), with the caveats mentioned above.

> "Although the text suggests that this may be related to low merger rates, the discrepancy is very large even in portions of the distribution where the true merger rate exceeds (1,\mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}})." 

As the Referee pointed out above, PixelPop performs worse in the negative region of the Xeff distribution. As discussed in lines 328-334 and 483-489 of the original manuscript, and in lines 328-334 and 492-500 of the current version of the manuscript, we note that away from the peak (e.g., for Xeff<-0.15), the discrepancy between the truth and the inferred merger rate is not necessarily driven by low merger rates. Rather, it is driven by low detectable rates (i.e., merger rates * detectability), and therefore by the lack of detections in that region. In fact, for  Xeff < -0.15, we calculate the expected number of detections over the analysis livetime (N_exp) to be ~1.4%_{-0.8%}^{+0.9%} of the total number of detections (i.e., 5_{-3}^{+2} sources) for both 3D and Hybrid PixelPop. One may wonder why the inferred N_exp is not necessarily ≤1 but can instead be O(1). In Appendix A of this reply, we include a toy example to show that, in regions without detections, the inference is driven by the integrated expected detection counts, so N_exp need not be ≤1 but can be O(1). We have also adapted the manuscript accordingly in lines 327 and 441. 

The Referee raises an excellent point in that some regions (e.g., the few bins immediately to the left of the Xeff peak) have an inferred merger rate that exceeds 1,\mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}}. For Xeff < 0, we find N_exp=56_{-16}^{+17} and N_exp=57_{-16}^{+18} for 3D and Hybrid PixelPop, respectively. Since only O(1) detections are expected for Xeff < -0.15, most of the events with inferred negative Xeff come from the region where -0.15 < Xeff < 0 (i.e., the three bins next to the peak). We are aware of this limitation of PixelPop. In the region where -0.15 < Xeff < 0, the inferred merger rate doesn't drop faster due to the underlying assumptions of the PixelPop prior. In particular, PixelPop assumes a smooth coupling in log-space between nearest neighbors, which makes it harder to infer sharp drops in the merger rate. Specifically, drops in the merger rate of several orders of magnitude (e.g., from the peak at Xeff=0 to the near-zero merger rate on the negative side of the distribution) are heavily penalized by the smoothing. Even though this smoothing may prevent some sharp features from being inferred, not having any smoothing would result in unreasonably noisy distributions instead. We have expanded our discussion in lines 500-511 to clarify this point and to discuss in more detail the limitations of non-parametric models.

As the Referee correctly points out later on in their review, these are intrinsic modeling choices characteristic of most non-parametric methods. In follow-up work, we are studying how these decisions affect modeling. In particular, we are exploring the prospect of identifying sharp features in source properties that are typically well-measured (e.g., primary and secondary mass) by analyzing simulated populations with sharp truncations in the mass distribution. For example, Figure 2 (attached to this reply) shows the probability density of logarithmic primary (row 1) and secondary (row 2) mass inferred by PixelPop for three underlying populations simulated under different parametric models (dashed gray) based on fits to GWTC-4. The columns show the analyses of 10 different catalog realizations, each containing 150 (blue) or 300 (red) events. The solid black lines represent the 90% posterior credible region inferred by PixelPop on GWTC-4. In the left panel (Single Power Law + 2 Peaks + Cutoff), where the underlying population has a sharp cutoff in secondary mass, PixelPop recovers a declining merger rate, but not a strict mass cutoff. These results motivate our ongoing work to quantify the conditions under which PixelPop can faithfully recover sharp truncations.

> "Moreover, even for Xeff> 0, the improvement over the parametric models does not seem as clear-cut as the paper implies."
> "Some key features of the population seem to be better recovered by the parametric model. The true Xeff distribution is strongly asymmetric. The parametric models successfully recover this feature, yielding a posterior that is confidently asymmetric. By contrast, many of the non-parametric reconstructions appear broadly consistent with an isotropic (symmetric) distribution."

We agree with the Referee that the Peak+Tukey parametric model captures the asymmetric behavior of the true Xeff marginal distribution. But, again, this feature can be recovered because we chose a parametric model that matches the truth. However, in real data, the appropriate functional form is not known in advance, and adopting an overly restrictive parametric model can introduce appreciable bias. As we mentioned above, this would be the case if one were to use the default LVK Gaussian model for Xeff, which lacks the flexibility to reproduce the observed asymmetry (purple curve in Fig. 1 attached to this response). 

Additionally, we agree that the "wings" of the 3D and Hybrid PixelPop Xeff distributions for Xeff <-0.15 and Xeff > 0.5 appear somewhat symmetric. This is a symptom of the absence of detections in these regions. However, we do see an improvement over the Peak+Tukey parametric model in the data-driven region 0 < Xeff < 0.5. For instance, at Xeff = 0.3, we find that the true merger rate is 14 \mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}} (evaluated at z=0.2). This is outside of the 90% credible interval of the parametric model (7_{-2}^{+3} \mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}}) but within the 90% credible region of the 3D PixelPop Xeff posterior distribution (8_{-4}^{+8} \mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}}) and the Hybrid PixelPop Xeff posterior distribution (9_{-4}^{+7} \mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}}). 

Furthermore, we note that PixelPop still recovers a significant asymmetry in the Xeff distribution. Because the merger-rate density is shown on a logarithmic scale, the peak near Xeff=0 corresponds to a prominent feature, rather than an approximately flat distribution. To quantify the asymmetry in the inferred distribution, we calculate the skewness for the Xeff 3D and Hybrid PixelPop marginal both at z=0.2 and z=0.8 in the region -0.5 < Xeff < 0.5. We consider a symmetric range around zero but restrict it to a narrower domain than -1 < Xeff < 1, since this narrower region contains more than 99% of our parameter estimation (PE) samples. In Table 1 (attached), we present two skewness estimates. Row 1 shows the skewness of the posterior-median Xeff distribution (i.e., the solid lines in Fig. 6). Row 2 shows the median and 90% central credible interval of the skewness of the Xeff marginal for each PixelPop posterior draw. In all cases, the posterior-median (row 1) has a considerable right skewness, and the median skewness across PixelPop draws (row 2) is also positive. Although symmetry is not ruled out, we find that more than 80% of the PixelPop samples in the 3D analysis (85% in the Hybrid PixelPop analysis) have positive skewness. We also calculate the mean of the Xeff 3D and Hybrid PixelPop marginals over the range -0.5 < Xeff < 0.5 (row 3), since it is better constrained than the skewness. In more than 94.6% of posterior draws for 3D PixelPop (97.3% for Hybrid PixelPop), the inferred mean excludes zero, indicating a preference for a non-isotropic distribution about the peak and ruling out isotropy at 94.6% (97.3%) significance. Therefore, we conclude that the nonparametric distribution still captures the asymmetry of the true Xeff distribution (albeit less noticeable than under our particular choice of the Peak+Tukey parametric model, as the Referee pointed out). 


> "This raises the possibility that the non-parametric method may simply lack sufficient flexibility to recover the true structure in regions with reasonably high merger rates. Any non-parametric framework necessarily depends on a set of internal modeling choices—such as binning schemes, kernel widths, or basis resolutions—and these can limit the model’s ability to capture sharp or highly asymmetric features if not chosen appropriately. In other words, also non-parametric models misspecified and possibly perform worse than parametric models. A more thorough exploration of how modeling choices affect the non-parametric reconstruction would help clarify whether the observed discrepancies reflect a true limitation of (non-)parametric approaches, suboptimal hyperparameter choices, or simply the amount of data available."

The Referee is correct: it is true that some nonparametric models (for instance, those that neglect correlations) can produce biased results, which is one of the reasons why we believe PixelPop is an important step forward in population inference.

Additionally, the Referee raises another excellent point: all models depend on modeling choices, as we commented above, and PixelPop is no exception. Even though we infer the optimal hyper-hyperparameters of PixelPop (i.e., the parameters of the ICAR prior), it still struggles in some scenarios. For instance, when the true distribution exhibits sharp drops in the merger rate. We are working to quantify and assess when these limitations pose problems in future work. In particular, as mentioned above, we are studying the limitations of non-parametric models in the context of inferring sharp cutoffs using PixelPop. Additionally, we are exploring the use of mixture models combining PixelPop and parametric models to leverage their advantages in tandem. However, despite the limitations of non-parametric models, we believe PixelPop constitutes a step forward, especially for inferring multi-dimensional correlations.

The Referee suggests that additional tests might help assess whether the discrepancies between the true and inferred distributions reflect an intrinsic limitation of the non-parametric approach. We hope that the discussion above shows that we do understand the limitations of PixelPop and why they occur, and appropriately caveat them in the paper.  While we agree that additional studies could be informative, carrying them out would require substantial additional computational resources, as well as another significant time delay (for a paper that was submitted in July), and we believe it might be better suited to a dedicated follow-up study on the limitations of PixelPop (and related non-parametric methods). In fact, we are already exploring some of these limitations in future work, as we mentioned above, in the context of analyzing sharp truncations in the mass distribution and using mixtures of PixelPop with parametric models.

Appendix A: Why N_exp≈O(1) instead of N_exp≤1 in regions without detections.
----------------------------------------------------------------------

To study why we measure N_exp ≈ O(1) instead of N_exp ≤ 1 in regions without detections, we consider a toy population: we draw N_obs = 100 events from a truncated Normal distribution with support between x ∈ [-2, 2]. We ran our inference changing the domain over which PixelPop is defined. In particular, we restricted PixelPop to the following ranges: [-3, 3], [-6, 6], [-9, 9], and [-12, 12], keeping the bin density fixed (i.e., same number of bins per unit x). Figure A.1 shows the inferred dN/dx for each PixelPop range we consider. The solid line shows the posterior median while the colored regions enclose the central 90% credible intervals. Note that the behavior of the tails is sensitive to the adopted domain. 

As we show in Figure A.2, we find that the expected number of detections outside the support of the truncated Normal, N_exp(x ∉ [-2, 2]), is O(1) for all the PixelPop ranges we consider. Therefore, even though the behavior of the tails is different for each PixelPop range, the inferred N_exp is always O(1) when integrating over the relevant domain. When we vary the observing time T_obs and number of observed events N_obs proportionally (T_obs ∈ {10^0, 10^1, 10^2, 10^3} and N_obs ∈ {10^2, 10^3, 10^4, 10^5}), we still see that N_exp(x ∉ [-2, 2]) is consistently O(1), as we show in Figure A.3, corresponding to an increasingly smaller fraction of the inferred population. 

These experiments, together with the fact that N_exp enters the gravitational-wave likelihood directly, suggest an "invariance principle": in regions where the true population does not have any support, we should measure N_exp ≈ O(1), regardless of the number of observations in the catalog or particular analysis settings. 

We argue the same principle holds for Xeff < -0.15 in our inferred Xeff distribution: the lack of detections informs the measurement N_exp≈O(1), and the detector sensitivity and observing time set the implied merger rate.
