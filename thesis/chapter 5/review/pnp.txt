- I suggest changing ‘[found] a preference for small natal spins for BHs born in the field’ to 'a preference for small natal spins for BHs born in isolation', as first generation BHs for the GC and NSC channels are also given the specified natal spin in the models used, while isolated BHs with prior spin-up of the stellar core due to tidal interactions are born with higher spins.

- Are there further details on the normalising flow used for the sensitivity estimation when doing mock PE? - The statement that CE and SMT channels ‘only differ in the way mass is transferred between components of the binary’ is not entirely true. They correspond to different parts of initial parameter space of the binary population, and their post-mass transfer evolution will also be different. I suggest adding some discussion on this or softening this statement.

-- We softened the sentence a bit: "We attribute this result to \ac{CE} and \ac{SMT} being field channels that share many evolutionary steps, even though they differ in the way mass is transferred between components of the binary."


- With real observations, could the similarity analysis be used to compare any population synthesis model to the PixelPop inference results? Would there be biases resulting from multiple channels present in the real observations?

-- For your first question: Yes, in principle, the similarity metric can be applied to real observations to compare any population-synthesis model with the PixelPop posteriors. However, since real data is most likely a result of a mixture of channels, you would need to adapt the similarity metric to include branching fractions. Otherwise, as you suggest, you would have biases from multiple channels present in real observations.

At O4 sensitivity, we already struggle to distinguish between single-channel populations (e.g., purely CE vs purely SMT). Distinguishing multiple channels in real data will be even more challenging (we added this caveat to the conclusions, thanks for the great question!).
