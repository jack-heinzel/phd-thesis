Report of the Referee -- XG11014/Alvarez-Lopez

The study investigates how different formation pathways for merging
black-hole binaries leave measurable patterns in their masses, spins,
and redshifts. Because traditional population models often assume
overly simple, uncorrelated distributions, they can lead to biased
conclusions about the underlying astrophysics. To address this, the
authors use PixelPop, a flexible Bayesian non-parametric framework
designed to capture complex, multidimensional correlations in the
population without imposing strong assumptions.

I believe the results of this work are technically impeccable. I was
not fully convinced, however, that some of the conclusions were
actually supported by the analysis or figures shown (see below). I
would appreciate if the authors clarified my concerns, before I can
recommend the paper for publication. If these are resolved, I think
the work is suitable for a high impact journal as PRX as it might
demonstrates a result that might have to be taken into account by the
community working on population inference.

The authors do not apply their method to real data, including O4.
However, using real observations would not strengthen their
conclusions and would be outside the scope of the work. Their main
argument relies on analyzing a population where the true parameter
correlations are known, in order to test whether their method can
recover those correlations, and whether simple parametric models
produce a bias.  Since we do not know the actual correlations in the
observed population, only a mock analysis can properly demonstrate
this point.

My main concern is with the conclusion that the non-parametric models
recover the true distribution, whereas the parametric models do not.
Part of this argument relies on examining the posterior distribution
of Xeff. The authors focus on the positive region (paragraph
beginning on line 385), where it is indeed the case that the
parametric reconstruction fails to capture the true behavior. However,
on the negative side of the distribution, Fig. 4 shows that the
parametric model reproduces the true distribution almost perfectly,
whereas Fig. 5 indicates that the non-parametric model performs
noticeably worse. Although the text suggests that this may be related
to low merger rates, the discrepancy is very large even in portions of
the distribution where the true merger rate exceeds
(1,\mathrm{Gpc^{-3},yr^{-1}}). Moreover, even for Xeff> 0, the
improvement over the parametric models does not seem as clear-cut as
the paper implies. 

Some key features of the population seem to be better recovered by the
parametric model. The true Xeff distribution is strongly asymmetric.
The parametric models successfully recover this feature, yielding a
posterior that is confidently asymmetric. By contrast, many of the
non-parametric reconstructions appear broadly consistent with an
isotropic (symmetric) distribution.

This raises the possibility that the non-parametric method may simply
lack sufficient flexibility to recover the true structure in regions
with reasonably high merger rates. Any non-parametric framework
necessarily depends on a set of internal modeling choices—such as
binning schemes, kernel widths, or basis resolutions—and these can
limit the model’s ability to capture sharp or highly asymmetric
features if not chosen appropriately. In other words, also
non-parametric models misspecified and possibly perform worse than
parametric models. A more thorough exploration of how modeling choices
affect the non-parametric reconstruction would help clarify whether
the observed discrepancies reflect a true limitation of
(non-)parametric approaches, suboptimal hyperparameter choices, or
simply the amount of data available.
